<p>The <b>Carnegie Museum of Art</b>, abbreviated <b>CMOA</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Art_museum" title="Art museum">art museum</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oakland_(Pittsburgh)" title="Oakland (Pittsburgh)">Oakland</a> neighborhood of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>. The museum was founded in 1895 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_people_from_the_Pittsburgh_metropolitan_area" title="List of people from the Pittsburgh metropolitan area">Pittsburgh-based</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrialist" title="Industrialist">industrialist</a> <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Self-published_sources" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on a self-published source. (March 2019)">self-published source?</span></a></i>]</sup> It was the first museum in the United States with a primary focus on contemporary art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> As instructed by its founder at the inception of the Carnegie International in 1896, the museum has been organizing many contemporary exhibitions that showcase the "Old Masters of tomorrow".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnegie_Museum_of_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The museum's origins can be traced to 1886 with <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>'s initial concept:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PopSci_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-PopSci-4">[4]</a></sup> "I am thinking of incorporating with the plan for a library that of an art-gallery in which shall be preserved a record of the progress and development of pictorial art in America." Dedicated on November 5, 1895, the art gallery was initially housed in the Carnegie Libraries of Pittsburgh Main Branch in <a href="/wiki/Oakland,_California" title="Oakland, California">Oakland</a>.
</p><p>Carnegie envisioned a museum collection consisting of the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Masters" title="Old Masters">Old Masters</a> of tomorrow".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup> The museum received a major expansion in 1907 with the addition of the Hall of Architecture, Hall of Sculpture, and Bruce Galleries, with funds again provided by Carnegie.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Memorial_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Memorial-5">[5]</a></sup>
</p><p>Under the directorship of Leon Arkus, the Sarah Mellon Scaife Gallery (125,000 square feet) was built as an addition to the existing Carnegie Institute. Designed by architect <a href="/wiki/Edward_Larrabee_Barnes" title="Edward Larrabee Barnes">Edward Larrabee Barnes</a>, it first opened in 1974 and more than doubled the museum's exhibition space, plus added a children's studio, theater, offices, café, and bookstore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilson_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson-6">[6]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> art critic <a href="/wiki/John_Russell_(art_critic)" title="John Russell (art critic)">John Russell</a> described the gallery as an "unflawed paradise." The gallery has been renovated several times since its original creation, most recently in 2004.
</p><p>Today the museum continues Carnegie's love of contemporary art by staging the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_International" title="Carnegie International">Carnegie International</a> every few years. Numerous significant works from the Internationals have been acquired for museum's permanent collection including <a href="/wiki/Winslow_Homer" title="Winslow Homer">Winslow Homer</a>'s <i>The Wreck</i> (1896) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_A._McNeill_Whistler" title="James A. McNeill Whistler">James A. McNeill Whistler</a>'s <i>Arrangement in Black: Portrait of Señor Pablo de Sarasate</i> (1884).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collections_and_departments">Collections and departments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnegie_Museum_of_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Collections and departments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carnegie_Presepio.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" decoding="async" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Carnegie_Presepio.JPG/220px-Carnegie_Presepio.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Carnegie_Presepio.JPG/330px-Carnegie_Presepio.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Carnegie_Presepio.JPG/440px-Carnegie_Presepio.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carnegie_Presepio.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Neapolitan <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Presepio" title="Presepio">presepio</a></i> seasonally displayed at the Carnegie Museum of Art</div></div></div>
<p>The museum's curatorial departments include: Fine Arts (Contemporary Art, Works on Paper), Decorative Arts, Architecture, and Photography. The museum presents as many as 15 changing exhibitions annually. Its permanent collection comprises roughly 35,000 works and includes European and American decorative arts from the late seventeenth century to the present, works on paper, paintings, prints (notably Japanese prints), sculptures and installations. The museum has notably strong collections of both aluminum artifacts and chairs. Approximately 1,800 works are on view at any given time.
</p><p>The museum also maintains a large archive of negatives from African-American photographer <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charles_%22Teenie%22_Harris" title='Charles "Teenie" Harris'>Charles "Teenie" Harris</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">[1]</a></sup>
</p><p><span class="anchor" id="Heinz_Architectural_Center"></span>
<b>Heinz Architectural Center</b> - The collection includes works in architecture, landscape design, engineering, and furniture and interior design. The center's facilities includes 4,000 square feet of exhibition space and a library housing several thousand books and journals.
</p><p><b>The Hillman Photography Initiative</b> - The Initiative hosts a variety of projects including live public events, web-based projects, documentary videos, art projects, and writing. Yearly programming is determine by a group of five "agents" who plan and curate each 12 month cycle of works hosted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2704" data-file-width="1966" decoding="async" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG/220px-Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG/330px-Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG/440px-Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a> <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Portal_of_the_North_Transept_of_the_Cathedral_of_Saint-Andre_at_Bourdaux_at_CMArt.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cast of the north transept portal of the <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux_Cathedral" title="Bordeaux Cathedral">Bordeaux Cathedral</a> in the Hall of Architecture</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Ailsa Mellon Bruce Galleries (1907) – originally constructed display reproduction bronze casts from Pompeii and Herculaneum. Renovated in 2009, the galleries exhibit more than 500 objects representing American and European decorative arts from the <a href="/wiki/Rococo" title="Rococo">Rococo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">Neoclassical</a> periods of the 18th century to contemporary design and craft.</li>
<li>Hall of Architecture (1907) – Today the Hall of Architecture houses almost 140 full-size plaster casts of elements of buildings found in the ancient and classical civilizations of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_architecture" title="Ancient Egyptian architecture">Egypt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_architecture" title="Ancient Greek architecture">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman_architecture" title="Ancient Roman architecture">Rome</a>, and from <a href="/wiki/Romanesque_architecture" title="Romanesque architecture">Romanesque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_architecture" title="Renaissance architecture">Renaissance</a> Europe. As such, it is the largest collection of plaster casts of architectural masterpieces in America and one of the three largest in the world, along with those of the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_national_des_Monuments_Fran%C3%A7ais" title="Musée national des Monuments Français">Musée national des Monuments Français</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>.</li>
<li>Hall of Sculpture (1907) – modeled upon the <a href="/wiki/Parthenon" title="Parthenon">Parthenon</a>'s inner sanctuary, and originally created to house the museum's 69 plaster casts of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_art" title="Ancient Egyptian art">Egyptian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Near Eastern</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_sculpture" title="Ancient Greek sculpture">Greek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_sculpture" title="Roman sculpture">Roman sculpture</a>. Today it exhibits works from the permanent collections, with its balcony displaying decorative arts objects from the eighteenth to the twentieth century.</li>
<li>Heinz Architectural Center (1993) – dedicated to the collection, study, and exhibition of architectural drawings and models.</li>
<li>Scaife Galleries – Focused primarily on European and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/American_art" title="American art">American art</a> since 1850, but also including collections of <a href="/wiki/African_art" title="African art">African art</a>, classical and Egyptian art, and older European and American art.</li>
<li>Works on Paper Gallery</li>
<li>Forum Gallery – Located on the first floor of the museum just inside the Forbes Avenue entrance, this single room is dedicated to temporary exhibitions of contemporary art. It opened November 3, 1990 with support from the National Endowment of the Arts. The first exhibition, <i>Forum 1</i>, was a solo show of Jeff Wall. Subsequent exhibitions were numbered sequentially (for example, <i>Forum 40</i> featured Felix de la Concha). Unlike larger museum exhibitions, which can take up to three years to plan and execute, Forum shows come together relatively quickly, and are open to any curatorial staff's vision. In the words of Vicky Clark, a longtime curator at the museum, "The idea was to make sure that we had an exhibition of contemporary art set up at all times."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_programs">Educational programs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnegie_Museum_of_Art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Educational programs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Saturday art classes in the galleries of Carnegie Museum of Art have been conducted for over 75 years. Alumni of the program include <a href="/wiki/Andy_Warhol" title="Andy Warhol">Andy Warhol</a>, photographer <a href="/wiki/Duane_Michals" title="Duane Michals">Duane Michals</a>, and contemporary artist <a href="/wiki/Philip_Pearlstein" title="Philip Pearlstein">Philip Pearlstein</a>. The museum has classes specific to various age groups.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup>
</p>
